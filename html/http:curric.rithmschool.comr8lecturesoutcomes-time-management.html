Importance vs. Urgency

It can be helpful to group tasks along two dimensions:
importance and urgency.

Urgent tasks are those that demand your attention right now.

Important tasks are those that most contribute to your long-term growth and development.

Scheduling Time for Deep Work

Deep Work vs. Shallow Work

From Deep Work: Rules for Focused Success in a Distracted World

Deep Work: Professional Activities performed in a state of distraction-free concentration that push your cognitive capabilities to their limit. These efforts create new value, improve your skill, and are hard to replicate.

Shallow Work: Noncognitively demanding, logistical-style tasks, often performed while distracted. These efforts tend not to create much new value in the world and are easy to replicate.

Pomodoro Technique

The Pomodoro Technique is emphasizes bursts of dedicated focus on a task, followed by a short breaks.

It can be broken down into the following steps:

  1. Pick a task to work on.
  2. Set a timer for a fixed amount of time (typically 25 minutes).
  3. Work only on your task while the timer runs.
  4. When the timer rings, stop working and take a short break (typically 5 minutes).
  5. Repeat steps 1 through 4. This sequence is sometimes called a pomodoro.
  6. (Optional.) After every third or fourth pomodoro, take a longer break.

Why pomodoro?

This technique is called the pomodoro technique because Francesco Cirillo, the originator of the technique, originally used a kitchen timer shaped like a tomato.

If youâd like to try this technique, there are plenty of apps you can use. There are also Chrome extensions you can install which will help with this technique by disabling your access to time-wasting websites (e.g. Facebook, Twitter, etc.) while youâre in the middle of a pomodoro. You can find one such example here.

Flowtime Technique

The Flowtime Technique is similar to the pomodoro technique, but is task-based, not time-based.

It can be broken down into the following steps:

  1. Pick a task to work on. This task should have a well-defined end. If not, you should think about how you can scope the task more clearly.
  2. Record the time you start on the task, and work for as long as you can on it.
  3. When you need a break, record your stop time, how long you worked, and how long youâre going to take a break (as long or as short as you need).
  4. (Optional.) Record whether or not youâre stopping work because of an interruption.
  5. Repeat steps 1 through 4.

Pomodoro vs. Flowtime

With the pomodoro technique, you take breaks after set amounts of time. With the flowtime technique, the goal is to take breaks after the completion of specific tasks (this is why itâs important to select tasks that have defined end goals). Some people gravitate towards one approach over the other.

The nice thing about flowtime is that you can use it to gather data on your own work habits, if this isnât something youâve done much thinking about. For example, you can use it to detect when during the day youâre the most focused, or how long it typically takes you to complete tasks youâll be doing repeatedly (e.g. filling out job applications).

You can read more about the flowtime technique in these two Medium posts: The Flowtime Technique, and The Flowtime Technique Cheat Sheet.

Productivity Journal

If the previous two techniques feel too prescriptive, another technique is to maintain a productivity journal.

The idea here is simply to take a few minutes at the end of the day to record everything you did that day.

Obviously, some days will be more productive than others. But keeping a record increases your accountability to yourself, and can be a helpful reference on days when youâre feeling unmotivated.
