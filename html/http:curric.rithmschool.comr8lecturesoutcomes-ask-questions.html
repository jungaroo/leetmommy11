Question Formulation Technique

The Question Formulation Technique is just that - a technique for generating lots of questions on a specific topic.

It was developed as a tool for teachers to help students ask questions.
But it can be used whenever you need to generate questions on a topic.

History of the Question Formulation Technique

The Question Formulation Technique was developed by Luz Santana and Dan Rothstein, and has been adopted by hundreds of thousands of educators across North America.

According to them, âThe skill of being able to generate a wide range of questions and strategize about how to use them effectively is rarely, if ever, deliberately taught. In fact, it has too often been limited to students who have access to an elite education. Our goal is to democratize the teaching of an essential thinking and learning skill that is also an essential democratic skill.â

For more on the technique, you can check out their book, Make Just One Change: Teach Students to Ask Their Own Questions. You can also read more about the highlights of the technique here.

Steps in the Question Formulation Technique

  • Decide on a question focus (i.e. what are you trying to ask questions about?)
  • For five minutes, generate as many questions as you can about the question focus.
    • Ask as many questions as you can.
    • Do not stop to discuss, judge, or answer the questions.
    • Write down every question exactly as it is stated.
    • Change any statement into a question.
  • Categorize your questions.
  • Prioritize your questions.

Question Focus

A question focus is a simple stimulus to be used to generate questions. It can be a short sentence, a phrase, even an image.
It should not itself be a question.

Examples (from here):

  • Your rights are protected by the constitution
  • Defeating math anxiety
  • The rise of renewable energy

Categorizing Questions

Once you have a list of questions, it can be helpful to group them in terms of open-ended and closed-ended questions.

Prioritizing Questions

As a final step, choose your top three questions according to whatever set of priorities you choose.

Here are a few different examples of ways you could prioritize:

  • Most important
  • Most time-sensitive
  • Will give you the most insight into the company
